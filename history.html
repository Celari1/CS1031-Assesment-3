The Romans

In the 6th century a Roman army had trooped through the Dunbane Parish. They left behind the reminants of 3 camps. They also left a signal station, a small fort and traces of their path north. Following this path north you will find one of the best preserved Roman forts in the United Kingdom, Where you can still view their massive defences.

The Foundation of Dunblane

Within Scotland, Dunblane is among the oldest of settlements. The town got its name from St Blane during the late 6th and 7th century. St Blane's exact relationship with the town is unclear, Although from the first written recording of the town, which was during the 10th century, it is refered to as Dulblaan or Blane's meadow. Some question if the Laighhills Park which is located along the river Allen on the edge of town could be the meadow reffered to.

Dunblane Cathedral

The Cathedral has been built on the site of a much earlier building which may have been constructed as early as the 9th century. You can still see parts of the earlier building in the first four storeys of the tower in the Cathedral, It has been built in Romanesque style and is estimated to have been built in around 1150 although some believe it was built much earlier in 1070. This makes it one of Scotlandâ€™s oldest buildings.

The current Cathedral was constructed in the 13th century by two Bishops. Bishop Clement started the build during 1232-1258 and was finalised by Bisop Robert de Prebenda during 1258-1284. The two Bishops built it all in the same style and now is an attractive sight. Although during its early years after being built the Cathedral was much more adorned with bejewelled alters and many colourful paintings were hung around the walls.

In around 1600 the roof of the nave ended up collapsing and was left a ruin for many years, until early 1890's when a highly successful restoration took place. The cathedral was managed to be restored without losing any of it's mediaeval character. The building is currently still in use for congregations and is open daily to visit.

A Stroll through the Old Town of Dunblane

There are many nice sites to see throughout Dunblane with the mixture of 18th century, Victorian and contemporary buildings. The Old Town is along the Old Great North Road from Bridgend, to the High Street, round the Cathedral, up the Braeport and through Ramoyle.

There are many sites to see across this route such as:

  • The Leighton Library which is open to the public during the summer and was built in the late 1680's to house the library of Bishop Robert Leighton, which he gave to Dunblane along with payment to built the library
  • On the corner facing the Cathedral you can find the Dean's House with some of the building dating in the early 17th century. The house currently holds the Dunblane Museum which has just been revamped and modernised
  • On Braeport you will find the old Victorian school, which is now known as the Braeport Centre. The building has 3 large classrooms where the teachers would have classes of up to 90 pupils in each.

The Battle of Sheriffmuir

The Battle of Sheriffmuir took place on the 13th of November 1715. It was held three miles north east of Dunblane and directly south of the Sheriffmuir Inn. You can go see the site of the battle either on foot or by car. The pinnacle of the Jacobite Rebellion in 1715 was this battle. The Jacobite army consisted of 8,000 men who were lead by the Earl of Mar charged on the Redcoat army which only had numbers of 2,500. The Highlanders managed to defeat the Redcoats on their right but the left posed a further challenge. The Jacobites were untimately defeated by the Government Army. The Jacobites ended up retreating to Perth and soon disbanded their army.

Dunblane's Andy and Jamie Murray

The 5th of August 2012 was an exciting day for Dunblane when Andy Murray won the gold medal at the Olimpics for tennis. He then followed it up on the 9th of September by winning the US Open, The first Grand Slam title won by a British playing since 1936. Dunblane has since erected a golden postbox to commemerate the gold medal won by Andy and has been visited by thousands since. On the 7th of July 2013 the town once again saw Andy making history by winning the Wimbledon men's final in 3 straight sets against the then world number 1 player Novak Djokovic. Later on in November 2015, Andy and his brother Jamie contributed to the winning of the Davis Cup for the first time in 79 years. Next year Jamie Murray became the world's top ranked mens doubles player after grand slam successes at the Australian and US Opens with partner Bruno Suares. Andy then went on to win Wimbledon again and a gold medal at the Rio Olympics. This was followed by a run of ATP tour wins which made him the number 1 mens singles player in the ATP rankings on the 7th of November 2016. To top off 2017 Andy was awarded a knighthood
